DOH, DEOG HEE

Doh President of Korea maritime & Ocean University

President Deong Hee Doh has a Bachelor’s degree from the Measurements and Control major in Korean Maritime and Ocean University, a Master’s degree from the Measurements and Control in Korean Maritime and Ocean University, and a Doctor’s degree from the Department of Mechanical Engineering from Tokyo University. He was appointed as a professor of Korean Maritime and Ocean University in 1995 and has served as the dean of the College of Engineering from 2015 to 2017 and served the head of the Ocean Academy

Explore the Latest in Maritime Research

Know more about the research at KMOU


Rechargeable batteries like lithium-ion batteries (LIBs) are seeing a surge in demand as technologies like electric propulsion ships and other vehicles become increasingly popular. However, sodium-ion batteries (SIBs) are a more sustainable alternative albeit thermodynamically unstable with graphite—the usual anode material. Researchers at KMOU, led by Dr Jun Kang, have developed a “doped” carbon-based anode that helps SIBs to surpass the performance of LIBs.


Indirect carbonation is popularly used as a technique to store CO2 and reduce its levels in the atmosphere. But, this technique requires the use of expensive solvents, thus making its commercial usage impractical. KMOU researchers, led by Prof Myoung-Jin Kim, have come up with a cost- effective approach to indirect carbonation using costless seawater and an alkali industrial by product.


Owing to the high demand of Cleaner shrimp, an ornamental marine animal, artificial breeding involves speeding up the sexual reproduction via eyestalk removal. But, this process is not animal- friendly and reduces the economic value of the shrimp. To this end, a team at KMOU, led by Cheol Young Choi, have shown an alternative method to promote sexual reproduction in shrimps, that is, red light illumination, making this process feasible and more humane.


Divers use rescue ropes to secure return paths underwater. To ensure visibility in turbid and deep waters, LED-powered side-emitting optical fiber ropes have begun to be used. But, attempts to maximize the brightness of these ropes have hit a roadblock. Now, scientists at KMOU, led by Prof Gyung-Suk Kil, have attempted to clear the road, opening the path to the future of marine activities.


Microbial electrolysis cells are devices used to produce high-purity hydrogen from organic matter. Inside these cells, exchange of charged particles occurs via a polymeric membrane. However, “biological fouling” or the accumulation of organic matter on the membrane surface limit the efficiency of these cells. Recently, scientists of the Republic of Korea found a way to overcome this, by developing a high performance membrane resistant to biofouling.
